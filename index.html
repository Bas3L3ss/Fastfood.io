What is fast food?

Fast food, or "fastfood" in English, refers to quickly prepared and served dishes. It encompasses meals with expedited service, often featuring pre-cooked ingredients served in take-away packaging. This compact, aromatic, and visually appealing cuisine caters predominantly to young people and teenagers in urban areas. A popular trend in big cities, fast food aligns with the modern, fast-paced lifestyle of the youth, seen as a convenient and fitting choice for their on-the-go routines.

Fast food establishments thrive on efficiency, streamlining the culinary process to meet the demands of a bustling clientele. The menu typically includes a variety of options, from burgers and fries to wraps and salads, providing a quick fix for hunger pangs. The standardized recipes and assembly-line approach contribute to consistent taste and speedy service.

McDonald's history

Founded in 1940 by Richard and Maurice McDonald, McDonald's has evolved from a small barbecue restaurant in San Bernardino, California, to a global fast-food giant. Ray Kroc, a Multimixer milkshake machine sales agent, joined in 1954, transforming the brand into a franchise powerhouse. The iconic Golden Arches officially became the symbol in 1963. McDonald's introduced the Big Mac in 1968 and the Happy Meal in 1979, solidifying its status as a cultural phenomenon.

KFC's history

Founded in 1930 by Colonel Harland Sanders, Kentucky Fried Chicken (KFC) originated as a roadside restaurant in Corbin, Kentucky. Sanders' secret blend of 11 herbs and spices contributed to the distinctive flavor of his fried chicken. The franchise model emerged in 1952 when he partnered with Pete Harman in Salt Lake City. By 1964, KFC had expanded internationally, marking its presence in Canada and the United Kingdom. In 1966, the company was acquired by Heublein Inc.
